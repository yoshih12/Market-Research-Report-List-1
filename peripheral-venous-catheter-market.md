<p><p>A Peripheral Venous Catheter (PVC) is a medical device that is inserted into the peripheral vein to deliver fluids and medications directly into the bloodstream. PVCs are typically made of materials such as polyurethane or silicone, and they come in various sizes to accommodate different patient needs.</p><p>The market for PVCs is experiencing robust growth due to several factors. Firstly, the increasing prevalence of chronic diseases such as diabetes and cardiovascular diseases is driving the demand for peripheral venous access. Additionally, the rising number of surgical procedures and the growing aging population are contributing to market growth. Moreover, advancements in catheter materials and design, which enhance the ease of insertion and reduce the risk of infection, are further propelling market expansion.</p><p>The demand for PVCs is also being driven by the need for safer and more efficient vascular access devices. <p><p>The peripheral venous catheter market consists of three types: temporary catheter, short-term catheter, and long-term catheter. Temporary catheters are used for a brief period, usually up to 72 hours, to administer medications or fluids. Short-term catheters are used for a slightly extended duration, ranging from a few days to a few weeks. Long-term catheters are designed for extended use, typically for several weeks to months. These catheters are used to deliver medications, draw blood, or provide nutrition, and can remain in the patient's vein for an extended period.</p></p>
